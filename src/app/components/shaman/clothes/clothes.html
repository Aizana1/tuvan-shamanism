Chapter IV: Attributes of Tuvan Shamans

Based on "Tuvan Shamans" by M.B. Kenin-Lopsan

The shaman's costume is not merely clothing; it is armor, a map of the cosmos, and a bird's body capable of flight. Without attributes, even a strong shaman is vulnerable.

Source: Управление Верховного Шамана VK

1. Khamnaar Ton (Ritual Cloak)

The cloak is a universe in itself. Explore its sacred elements.

Iyi Kara Kuskun

Two Black Ravens

Wooden ravens on shoulders. They act as scouts, flying ahead to find lost souls.

Aldy Chuuru

Lynx Fur

A strip of lynx leg fur along the sleeves. Can be replaced by wolf or fox.

Manchak

Fringe Bundle

156 leather cords. Vainshtein wrote: "These are the wings allowing the shaman to fly."

Source: National Museum of the Republic of Tuva

Chylan

Snakes

Shoulder Snakes (Egin chylan): 18 fabric snakes.
Shooting Snakes (Syr chylan): Metal chains with arrowheads to fight spirits.
Striped Snakes (Cheep chylan): 9 snakes carrying tobacco pouches.

Konguraalar

Bells & Whistles

15 metal tubes and bells on the back. Their sound creates a hypnotic rhythm during movement.

Shenek Bailaa

Richness of Elbow

Brocade pouch and 9 leather straps on the left sleeve with three white ribbons.

2. Khamnaar Bort (Shamanic Headdress)

"The headdress serves as protection from evil spirits and grants the shaman double vision."

Source: Управление Верховного Шамана VK

Copper Face (Khola Khaay, Khola Karak)

A copper nose and copper eyes are sewn onto the front. Surrounded by red fabric circles, they give the headdress the appearance of a mythical creature.

Copper Horns (Ches Myiys)

Stylized copper horns (~15cm) forged by a blacksmith. They protect against evil spirits. Historically, horned headdresses have a deep tradition dating back to the Iron Age.

Eagle Feathers (Sannash)

The main decoration consists of 40 large and 20 small eagle feathers. They represent the birds—spirit helpers. "The eagle spirit helps the shaman to fly."

Black Wings (Suuk-Kara)

Two long black feathers at the very front. They act as "fins" or rudders, helping the shaman overcome air resistance during his cosmic flight.

The Fringe (Borttun Khanazy)

The "wall of the hat" covers the eyes. Traditionally decorated with maral (deer) teeth or beads and checkerboard embroidery. It hides the shaman's human gaze from the spirits.

Source: Управление Верховного Шамана VK

3. Dungur & Orba (Drum & Beater)

"The Drum is the shaman's steed. The Beater is his whip. Together they transport him through the worlds."

The Drum (Dungur)

The Frame (Rim)

Made of larch wood. Before cutting the tree, a ritual is performed to feed the forest spirit. The bark is removed, and the board is bent into a circle.

The Skin (Dungurnun Algyzy)

Depending on the region, the skin of a mountain goat (*te*), maral deer, or roe deer is used. Western shamans painted it with cinnabar. The shaman calls the drum his "riding horse" (*te-khelge*).

The Handle (Tudazy)

The vertical bar inside. It often depicts the monstrous bird Khaan-Khereti or the shaman himself. The bottom features three snakes — the shaman's whip against enemies.

The Crossbar (Sheezhep)

A horizontal willow stick holding bronze bells and arrowheads.

Source: Ritual Practice

The Beater (Orba)

Material

Made from elk horn (Eastern Tuva) or pine/juniper root (Western Tuva). The beater is not just a stick; it is a sacred tool for divination.

Front Side (Orbanym Arny)

The striking side is covered with the skin of a bear or mountain goat.

Back Side (Orbanym Arty)

Decorated with carvings (often a pine tree with a bird) and metal rings. The rings jingle, adding another layer of sound to the ritual.

Divination Tool

The beater is used to predict the future. When thrown, if it lands fur-up, it is good luck. Wood-up means danger.
